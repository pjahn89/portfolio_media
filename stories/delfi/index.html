Delfi: Hedging as a shield

I built Delfi around a simple belief: stability is a prerequisite for growth. This is a first-person account of how I connected a family story about food security to algorithmic interest-rate risk management, grounded in Delfi’s SVB analysis.

Interest rate risk Derivatives hedging Yield curve dynamics Scenario simulation AI search over strategies
Perspective: founder Artifact: SVB report Output: hedge recommendation

Origin: rice prices, then capital

My grandfather served as an economic minister in post-war Korea. One of the jobs that mattered most was stabilizing the price of rice. If people fear they will not eat tomorrow, the entire economy becomes brittle. Stability is not a luxury. It is the platform that lets real growth happen.

I co-founded Delfi to pursue the same idea in modern markets. Not as speculation and not as a bet on being right about the future, but as hedging: a shield, not a sword. The motivation was also practical. Robust risk management has historically lived behind the walls of large banks. I wanted software to make that capability accessible, fast, and operational in everyday decision-making.

Why SVB became the proving ground

Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) collapsed in early March 2023 after a rapid loss of confidence and a bank run. Delfi’s report frames it as a textbook case of interest rate risk mismanagement. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

The core setup: a balance sheet tilted toward long-maturity securities on the asset side, paired with generous deposit-rate policies on the liability side. When rates rose quickly, mark-to-market losses overwhelmed the gains from higher net interest income, and disclosure catalyzed a run. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

Balance sheet intuition for a non-banker

Duration (informal): how sensitive a bond’s price is to changes in interest rates. Longer-maturity fixed-coupon bonds generally have higher duration, so they fall more when rates rise.

Deposit beta (informal): how much of market-rate moves pass through to what the bank pays depositors. The report estimates SVB’s beta at about 0.5 versus a national beta around 0.1. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

SVB’s assets were unusually concentrated in securities. The report summarizes the 2022 mix as roughly 57% securities, 35% loans, with the remainder in cash and other assets. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

The bigger issue was not only “lots of securities,” but “long securities.” The report shows most of the securities portfolio sitting at ten years or more maturity. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

The uncomfortable lesson of scenario thinking

A recurring failure mode in risk is not a lack of intelligence. It is a lack of imagination under constraint: people anchor to a narrow band of “reasonable” futures. Delfi’s report emphasizes this with a stark point. At the beginning of January 2021, its models put only about a 1.2% probability on the Fed funds rate reaching at least 4.75% over the next two years. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

The lesson I took is not “predict the tail.” It is “build a system that survives it.” The moment you stop trying to be right and instead try to be robust, the whole problem changes.

What “AI hedging” meant in practice

The report’s framing is pragmatic: simulate interest-rate scenarios, map them through an (extrapolated) balance sheet, and search the space of hedging actions for strategies that improve outcomes across scenarios. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

Why this is hard without automation: the yield curve can move in many shapes, not just “up” or “down.” The report highlights exposure to steepening where longer-maturity rates rise even while shorter-maturity rates remain low. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

The hedge Delfi’s AI would have recommended (as of Q1 2021)

In the SVB simulation, Delfi’s AI recommends a calibrated “spreading” strategy: long short-maturity Fed Funds futures, offset by short positions in longer-maturity Treasury futures. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

Results: robustness, not clairvoyance

The report’s headline outcome is simple: this hedging program substantially reduces performance volatility and flips the simulated outcome from loss to gain. It states that ex-ante volatility would have been reduced by 95%. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

Ex-post, the report summarizes performance as approximately a +$3.5B gain instead of a -$2B loss, and later reports a +$3.3B gain versus a -$2.0B loss within its simulation section. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

The story I want to tell here is not “we predicted the crisis.” It is that disciplined scenario thinking, paired with fast search over hedging actions, can turn fragile balance sheets into survivable ones.

Design constraints that mattered to me

Two constraints shaped how I think about this category of product.

Speed: risk insights have to be usable inside real decision cycles, not in a quarterly slide deck. If a hedge takes days to compute, it becomes political. If it takes minutes, it becomes operational. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}

Interpretability: the output has to translate to concrete trades and a clear narrative about what risks are being neutralized. The value is not just the number, but the logic it supports.
